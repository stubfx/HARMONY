# Behavior

*How the swarm is felt by the people inside it.*

---

## The premise

No one controls this.

That is not a limitation — it is the entire point. A single person holding their phone is not operating a remote control. They are becoming part of a living system, contributing a small and irreversible force to something that already exists and will continue to exist without them. Their gesture matters. It disappears into the whole.

This is the sensation we are designing for: participation without authorship.

---

## How the body speaks to the swarm

### Tilt — your personal wind

Hold the phone still and the swarm breathes on its own. Tilt it — forward, sideways, anywhere — and the portion of the field that belongs to you bends with it.

This is deliberately personal. Tilt is not averaged with anyone else's. Your orientation steers only your assigned slice of the field — the particles that carry your color. The rest of the swarm is untouched. You cannot move the whole field. You can only move your part of it.

Portrait is neutral. The moment you tip the phone, a current runs through your particles. Put it flat and they drift back into the shared current.

The phone is a plumb line. Your fragment of the swarm is the weather. You are somewhere in between.

### Touch — warmth, cold, and your own swarm

Where you press your finger on the screen does two things at once.

The **vertical position** determines the temperature of your presence. The top of the screen is cold — blue, distant, crystalline. The bottom is warm — amber, slow, close. Every active touch sends a temperature into the room. The swarm's color is the average of everyone's warmth. If the crowd is pressing down, the field glows amber. If they are pressed up high, it shifts toward ice. You are choosing a feeling, not a color. The swarm translates.

The **position itself** — not just the height — calls a piece of the swarm to you. Each person who joins is assigned a color and a slice of the particle field. Those particles are yours in color only: they live everywhere, mixed into the whole. But when you touch the screen, a small fraction of them teleport to wherever your finger is, as though the field is orienting toward you. Hold still and they collect. Move quickly and they scatter and regroup. Lift your finger and they drift back into the current.

You cannot isolate your swarm from the others. You can only pull it toward you, temporarily, in one place.

### Words — the ghostwriting

Type something and the swarm will write it. Not immediately, not cleanly — the letters form the way fog forms, density emerging from drift. The text is a landscape the particles inhabit, not a message they display.

Multiple people can write. The last word wins. What it means for the swarm to "write" a word someone typed on a phone in the dark — that is the question this piece lives inside.

---

## What the phone feels like to hold

The screen is dark. There are no instructions.

The only visible thing is a faint trace of the session — proof that this phone is tethered to the projection happening somewhere else in the room. This is enough. The person holding the phone does not need to know more.

They find the interactions through their body. Tilting. Pressing. The aura behind the screen shifts color as they touch — a small, private reflection of the temperature they are sending into the collective. They cannot see what the others are sending. They can only feel the gap between their contribution and the result.

That gap is where the piece lives.

---

## What "control" means here

The interaction is designed so that no single person can determine the outcome, but no single person's contribution is meaningless.

This is structurally similar to a murmuration. Each starling follows three simple rules and has no awareness of the global shape. The shape emerges anyway — coherent, alive, impossible to attribute to any individual.

We want the person holding the phone to feel like one starling. Not powerless. Not dominant. Present.

---

## What we are not making

We are not making a game. There is no score, no feedback loop that rewards correct behavior, no sense of winning or losing. Someone who does nothing — who just holds the phone — is still contributing their stillness.

We are not making a tool. Tools have a clear purpose and a correct use. This has neither. It has only this: a field of motion, and the aggregate of everyone who chose to touch it.

---

## The moment it works

The piece works when someone in the room with the projection notices the swarm drifting and looks around at the people with phones, wondering — who did that? And the answer is: all of us, and none of us, in proportions nobody will ever know.

That wonder is the work.
