# Behavior

*How the swarm is felt by the people inside it.*

---

## The premise

No one controls this.

That is not a limitation — it is the entire point. A single person holding their phone is not operating a remote control. They are becoming part of a living system, contributing a small and irreversible force to something that already exists and will continue to exist without them. Their gesture matters. It disappears into the whole.

This is the sensation we are designing for: participation without authorship.

---

## How the body speaks to the swarm

### Touch — your own swarm

Each person who joins is assigned a color and a slice of the particle field. Those particles are yours in color only: they live everywhere, mixed into the whole. When you move the joystick, a small fraction of them teleport to wherever the spawner is heading, as though the field is orienting toward you. Hold still and they collect. Move quickly and they scatter and regroup. Lift your finger and they drift back into the current.

The color picker at the top of the screen changes which hue your slice carries. You are choosing which fragment of the swarm is recognizably yours — for as long as you hold it.

Shake the phone and your particles lose their color. They dissolve back into the base tone of the field, indistinguishable from everything else. Touch the joystick again and the color returns.

You cannot isolate your swarm from the others. You can only pull it toward you, temporarily, in one place.

### Words — the ghostwriting

At certain moments in the story, the phone opens a space to write. A single input field appears on the screen, and for a few seconds it is yours.

Type something and the swarm will write it. Not immediately, not cleanly — the letters form the way fog forms, density emerging from drift. The text is a landscape the particles inhabit, not a message they display.

Then the field closes. The phone returns to what it was. What remains on the screen is not a transcript — it is a trace. What it means for the swarm to "write" a word someone typed on a phone in the dark — that is the question this piece lives inside.

### The vote — a choice that doesn't feel like a choice

At certain moments, the field splits into two. Two options appear on the screen and a countdown begins.

The options feel like a fork in the story. They are not. Both paths lead to the same next moment — the same structural beat in the narrative arc. What the vote decides is the texture of how we arrive there. The detail that makes the story feel personal.

Nobody knows what anyone else voted. Nobody knows if their vote swayed the result. The story continues regardless. This is the sensation we are designing for: agency without certainty.

---

## What the phone feels like to hold

The screen is dark. There are no instructions.

The only visible thing is a faint trace of the session — proof that this phone is tethered to the projection happening somewhere else in the room. This is enough. The person holding the phone does not need to know more.

They find the interactions through their body. Pressing. Moving. The aura behind the screen shifts color and intensity — a small, private reflection of the temperature and chaos they are sending into the collective. They cannot see what the others are sending. They can only feel the gap between their contribution and the result.

That gap is where the piece lives.

---

## What "control" means here

The interaction is designed so that no single person can determine the outcome, but no single person's contribution is meaningless.

This is structurally similar to a murmuration. Each starling follows three simple rules and has no awareness of the global shape. The shape emerges anyway — coherent, alive, impossible to attribute to any individual.

We want the person holding the phone to feel like one starling. Not powerless. Not dominant. Present.

---

## What we are not making

We are not making a game. There is no score, no feedback loop that rewards correct behavior, no sense of winning or losing. Someone who does nothing — who just holds the phone — is still contributing their stillness.

We are not making a tool. Tools have a clear purpose and a correct use. This has neither. It has only this: a field of motion, and the aggregate of everyone who chose to touch it.

---

## The moment it works

The piece works when someone in the room with the projection notices the swarm drifting and looks around at the people with phones, wondering — who did that? And the answer is: all of us, and none of us, in proportions nobody will ever know.

That wonder is the work.

---

## The Preshow

Before the piece begins, the screen is black. Not dormant — running, waiting. Every particle already exists; none of them are visible.

Then the first person scans the code and connects. Something stirs at the centre of the screen — a small cluster of particles, emerging from the middle of the dark. They drift outward. You can count them if you look. Ten percent of everything that will become the swarm.

The next person joins. Another ten percent appears — from the same place, the same centre, spreading into the field the first arrivals have already begun to explore. The screen accumulates.

This continues for as long as people keep arriving. Each connection wakes a fraction of what was already there but couldn't be seen. The swarm doesn't grow — it reveals itself. The dark between the particles is not absence. It is potential that hasn't been claimed yet.

By the time enough people have joined, the screen holds something moving and full. Nobody assembled it deliberately. It assembled in response to people walking into a room and deciding to be part of something.
